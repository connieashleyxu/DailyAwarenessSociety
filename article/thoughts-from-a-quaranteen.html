Thoughts from a QuaranTeen

Self

01/31/21

On Zoom University…

I’m sure many students can relate when I say Zoom fatigue is honestly so tough. Sometimes, I find myself falling into robotic routines where all I do is move from my bed to my desk to my kitchen. On those days that seem to blur together, things that once motivated me turn into distant thoughts.

There’s something about a Zoom call that seems so impersonal -- we see the faces of our professors and peers, but lack real connections. It’s weird to admit that I can’t imagine seeing my peers in an in-person setting. I often wonder if my virtual friends would be able to recognize me if we saw each other on the street.

Zoom University can be full of inconveniences like spotty wifi kicking you out of class, fear of being off mute on accident, or body aches from being hunched over a computer the whole day. I try not to even think of how fun in-person classes would have been because there’s really no point dwelling on what could’ve been.

On the bright side of things, Zoom University has proved to be quite helpful in numerous ways. It’s now easier than ever to get to class; sometimes I’ll literally just wake up and go to class in a matter of seconds. Moreover, class has been more accessible in terms of the fact that we can attend class from literally anywhere. Whether we are doing groceries, at the gym, or in bed.

I’m not sure if I’m the only one who does this, but if my schedule is tight, I’ll schedule multiple Zoom meetings at the same time (but only on rare occasions when no other time works!). Being able to multi-task and knock off multiple things off your checklist at the same time has been easier than ever.

Admittedly, I would rather have in-person classes and opportunities, but I’m genuinely content with my semester and a half of Zoom University.

On Social Life…

As a freshman going into a new school, I wasn’t sure how my social life would pan out. Would I have friends to ask class questions to? Would I even have friends?

I honestly think that I overthought a lot of my social life at the beginning of quarantine -- at the end of the day, everyone else is wondering the same thing, so everybody is avidly open to meeting new friends.

Looking back on it, I feel like I made more friends over the virtual setting than if classes were in person. I’ve found it easier to overcome that social anxiety when being virtual. Namely, my introduction to Clubhouse (an audio-only social app) has helped me a lot in terms of becoming more receptive to striking conversations with strangers.

I wouldn’t say I lost any friends, but I have become distant to a few of my friends. I don’t think its the fault of either party, our schedules just happened to not align/we thrived off of in-person connections.

Quite frankly, I miss my friends. While it seems like I’m always on a FaceTime with a friend, it’s definitely not the same as being in the same room.

On Self Improvement…

When everything was in-person, I found myself caught up in the nuances of others. I cared a lot about what other people were doing; where they were going; what new trendy restaurants are open; who people were with.

Since the quarantine began, I found myself become more independent yet more dependent. That sounds paradoxical, but let me offer an explanation:

I started caring less about what others were doing. I used to have severe FOMO -- I would never say no to a chance to hang out with someone (sometimes at the cost of my well-being). However, the more I remained in quarantine, I was able to distance myself from these mildly-toxic thoughts. It’s not necessarily that people stopped hanging out to be COVID-cautious, it’s mainly just because I learned to love myself during the quarantine. Learning to be satisfied with me was the best thing that could’ve happened during this quarantine.

On the flip side, I found myself more dependent on relationships that were important to me. I got a lot of joy from being with my family and close friends. Being able to have a support system during this quarantine was definitely a must. I noticed that I would text/call my friends more frequently. I think it’s important to take time for yourself, but also find balance and spend time with family and friends.
