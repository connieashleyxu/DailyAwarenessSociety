Westernized Education Breeds Institutional Racism

Equality

07/26/20

Take a moment to recollect and savor your childhood memories -- those blissful moments when we were so carefree, so unbothered, so oblivious. Remember your favorite bedtime stories? Do authors like Dr. Seuss and Roald Dahl ring a bell? Maybe you can even remember the moments your parents read aloud your favorite book as you drifted off to sleep.

For me, my bedtime stories weren’t your typical “Oh, the Places You’ll Go,” but rather memories my parents relived as they retold their childhood stories during the Cultural Revolution. I loved hearing their accounts -- it brought me closer to my Chinese background, but I didn't realize it back then. I remember thinking that their realities seemed more like fantasies as I never thought to actually put myself in their shoes.

“Soldiers rushed into our house one day and inspected every corner. The next day, 3 families that we had never met before moved in.”
“There was this beautiful young lady who had a little bit of a limp. The boys would kick her until she fell onto the cement. No one would ever help her because we were so terrified of the ruthless boys.”

It wasn’t until high school that I learned about the Cultural Revolution in my AP World History class. I had spent 9 out of my 12 years of education relearning the same bulk of American History that we had already been taught throughout elementary school.

“Christopher Columbus discovered America in 1492. Without him, we would not have the luxury of being here today.”
“The American plantation owners thrived off of the use of slaves; it was a needed evil to bring America to her point of prosperity.”

In all the 12 years of my education, my history courses were taught by white people with textbooks written by white people. Because the sources of my historical education were taught by those who have never experienced racism in the U.S., the amount of bias and skew placed on every history lesson was unnatural.

Flash forward to the present time, the issue of institutional racism is being amplified yet again. But, what long-term changes are we really seeing?

Not much. One of the many roots that fuel this mentality of degrading POCs in the U.S. starts with our education system.

Remember seeing textbook pictures of labels that designated what public utility people could use based on if they were “white” or “colored”? Remember the eerie images of the men hiding behind white hoods and gowns as they found their next murder victims just because they were black? These images hold very vivid memories for me** -- I was so disgusted that such blatant racism could be tolerated. I was led to believe that these things could never happen now because we recognize the negative impacts of segregation. After all, we learned that the government system we have today outlaws these horrid acts, right?

Not exactly. The sad reality is that there are just reiterations in our government to keep POCs, especially blacks, oppressed. Remember learning that the only American citizens who cannot vote are felons? What our educational system failed to teach us is that blacks are disproportionately targeted for crime. In other words, the ratio of black felons is greater than any other race.[1][2] Connecting the dots, with more blacks charged as felons, politicians are able to legally keep blacks from voting. With less representation at the polls, the cycle of systemic racism will persist.

Wholistically speaking, when our educational system fails to depict accurate representations of minorities and cultures outside the Eurocentric ideal, children cannot learn to understand and appreciate other customs. If we grow up only knowing “white” culture and we are shamed for looking different, whether it may be the color of our skin or the shape of our eyes, we are conditioned to believe westernized culture is superior.[3] This notion funnels the abasement of people who don’t fall into that classic 1950s White American ideal, which only perpetuates racism.

Only after committing to digging deeper into documentation and papers outside of school, have I realized that our education system ensures that we turn a blind eye to all the problems within our society that have never genuinely changed since the creation of the United States of America. That oblivion facilitated to us as young minds may not be so blissful if we were taught to take a step back and really simmer on the corruption bred into our society from the day we enter the education system at age 5.

While I recognize scrapping the entire educational system and rebuilding it from scratch is an unrealistic short-term goal, there are baby steps we can take to reform U.S. education. Advocating for diversity amongst the student population as well as the teacher population should be the first step we take*. Diversity will bring the ability to grasp different perspectives of history and culture to our education system. By building such environment, we can stop the cycle of breeding institutional racism to young and susceptible minds. Only then will we be able to see a glimpse of a future where all of our children are raised to be compassionate and genuinely educated individuals.

Take a moment to imagine that future -- one where the social constructs of race are mitigated. It’s almost impossible to visualize this future without picturing some fictional utopia, and that is perhaps what is the most haunting.
