Know My Name: A Summer Reading Book I Did Not See Coming

Arts & Entertainment, Society

08/16/20

Okay, first of all, I know this book review is embarrassingly late to the party. Although Know My Name was published just last year, most of us had at least heard about what happened when it made headlines in 2015: a Stanford swimmer raped a girl behind a dumpster at a frat party, the media focused too much on his clean track record and potential as an athlete, he was only sentenced to six months in county jail, wait hold on what the fuck how does raping someone equate to six months in jail. A classic example of white male privilege and a true embodiment of everything wrong with the criminal justice system and its handling of sexual assault. My friends and I had all read about it online, sent each other articles denouncing rape culture, retweeted posts supporting the victim, and shaken our heads at the system. But before long, much like any other devastating piece of news that makes its way to the trending page on Twitter, the story eventually faded from our conversations. When I found out the victim had released a memoir about the incident several years later, my interest slightly peaked. The book received raving reviews, international praise, and was an instant New York Times bestseller. And yet, I never got around to reading it (see: “gifted kid burnout”) until now, when it became my summer reading assignment for college. And I am so glad I did.

I think The Wrap’s review on the back cover summarizes it best: we are given “the privilege of knowing [the author] not just as Emily Doe, but as Chanel Miller the writer, the artist, the survivor, the fighter.”[1] It isn’t enough to just say that this book is beautifully written, because that wouldn’t do it justice. Miller writes in a way that plants the reader directly and firmly into her shoes, regardless of their own experiences with sexual assault, so that they are with her through every emotion, interaction, setback, and victory. When she wrote about anxiously waiting for the jury’s decision during the early stages of trial, my heart beat faster and my palms moistened, even though I had already known the result. When she wrote about feeling the need to protect her younger sister from harsh interrogations and court procedures even though she was the victim, my heart softened and somehow I understood, despite being the younger sibling in my family. It even feels weird to refer to her as “Miller” instead of “Chanel” as I write this, because it’s almost as if I know her personally and we’re already on a first name basis. If I were to highlight the sentences and paragraphs in the book that resonated especially strongly with me, there would be more yellow than white. But the following is worth mentioning:

“I was waiting to be knocked back down to size, to the small place I imagined I belonged. I had grown up in the margins; in the media Asian Americans were assigned side roles, submissive, soft-spoken secondary characters. I had grown used to being unseen, to never being fully known. It did not feel possible that I could be the protagonist. The more recognition I gained, the more I felt I was not supposed to be on the receiving end of so much generosity. Yet people kept pulling me up and up, until I heard from the highest house in our nation.”

To strike so deeply about an issue as dark and sensitive as rape takes a unique type of talent, one that Miller has mastered well as a writer; to connect it to external themes of race, self-image, and power dynamics is another thing entirely. It was through this in which I realized that she and I had gone through similar struggles even before her assault — that I could have been her, that sexual assault can happen to anyone. Miller is living proof that victims are multifaceted, dimensional human beings defined by much much more than their trauma.

Miller bares her soul and reveals her wounds openly and unabashedly through her writing, allowing us to step into her life for just a moment to look beneath the headlines and news reports. To understand the pain and loss that comes with every rape statistic we read about, and to recognize that the one isolated incident we hear about carries lifelong implications, not only on the victim but on those around her as well. Her strength shines through her vulnerability, and she literally and metaphorically takes the narrative back into her own hands. Her humor, wit, intelligence, and artistry firmly establish her as a force to be reckoned with, something that stands true to this day through the cartoons she creates and shares online.

Know My Name is the first book I’ve read, purely for my own enjoyment, in several years. Miller’s writing has inspired me to jump back into reading, and it has forever changed the way I view cases of sexual assault and rape. It should be on everyone’s reading list because it transcends themes of gender, age, race, and identity; further, as Jennifer Weiner of The New York Times writes, “it especially deserves to be read by the next generation of young men, the could-be Brocks and Elliots, who have grown up seeing women’s bodies as property to plunder, who believe that sex is their right.”[2] If we as a society are to effectively combat and overcome sexual violence and the customs and institutions that normalize it, we must believe and amplify those who have been most impacted, whether they are renowned writers like Chanel Miller, or simply our friends, relatives, and peers.
