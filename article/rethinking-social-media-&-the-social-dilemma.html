Rethinking Social Media & The Social Dilemma

Arts & Entertainment, Social

10/04/20

“The only two industries that call their customers users are illegal drugs and software.” - Edward Tufte

Recently, I watched the new Netflix documentary The Social Dilemma. Working with former tech executives to close the knowledge gap between AI and the general public, the documentary calls society to rethink their technology usage and abolish the business models that capitalizes off of tech's negative impacts.

Ironically, I initially heard about the film through AI algorithms and social media. While I was watching another show on Netflix, the recommendation for this show popped up on my screen. Shortly after, my friends had shared it with me via Facebook Messenger. Technology appears in every aspect of our lives. As I was watching the film, this statement proved itself over and over again.

In the tech industry, companies have specific teams allocated for growth marketing. Facebook, Instagram, Twitter, TikTok, Linkedin -- you name it. The purpose of growth is undoubtedly profit. When businesses start, it’s imperative to include in their model potential revenue streams, and this goes the same for established corporations as well. Though social media services are free for us consumers to download, they are the wealthiest companies to ever exist in history. Alphabet, the parent company of Google and Youtube, was worth 859.2 billion dollars in 2019. Facebook was worth 511.35 billion[1]. But how do these companies make so much when they aren’t even selling directly to the consumer? Because growth professionals market the very thing that is precious to all of us: time.

I’ve heard the sayings “time is precious” and “the only thing you can’t buy is time” since I was a kid. Yet, big tech has found the way to monetize our time. Likes, comments, photo tagging. When we see the notification that “someone tagged you in a photo,” we are inclined to click on it and see what we’ve been tagged in. Once we click on it, Facebook, Instagram, etc. now have control over our time, which advertisers will bid for and buy. This is a never ending cycle for the 3.8 billion users on social media[2], and there is no restraint to what is being sold. There could be an ad promoting that climate change is a hoax on one person’s screen or an ad promoting conspiracy theories like Pizzagate on another’s; there is no distinction between fact and fiction. Technology discovering a way to control our time is just a step to exploring what else they can control, such as our political beliefs. Naturally, we develop our ideologies through the information we are presented with, but because social media has the power to personalize our information the way we like it, they can skew our beliefs one way or another. As a result, America’s social fabric is becoming more polarized every single day[3]

Another big takeaway I got from the documentary was the idea that “nobody really knows what they are doing.” I remember being told this frequently when I attended talks from leading figures in different professional fields. The phrase is commonly used as a way to mitigate imposter syndrome, and I thought that this was a good thing. I don’t really know what I’m doing right now, but no one does either. However, I find it a bit concerning that the AI engineers have a high chance of not knowing what they are doing either. In the documentary, the creator of the Facebook like button never estimated that it would be used as a superficial measure of popularity that could spark depression and eating disorders. These creations are way too prevalent for designers to not fully understand, and this is a perfect example of why STEM is intertwined with humanities. Those in STEM must think about the impact of their creations before release.

As I am writing this article, I know that DAS will promote it through social media. Even more ironic is the fact that shortly after I watched that documentary, I returned quickly back to social media to share my thoughts with my friends. Software is so ingrained into our lifestyle that I can’t imagine life without it. But there exists a negative side to tech that engineers and non-engineers must both consider. Not all technology is inherently bad. Rather, it’s how we use it and the economic models that propagate the toxic usage of it. What I found most frightening about this documentary was that even though I knew the animations and storyline were being exaggerated, I could picture all of it happening in reality. We must reframe our usage and take down the markets that enable its toxicity now more than ever.

Action Items:

  • Watch The Social Dilemma (Available on Netflix)
  • Delete unused apps off your phone and turn off notifications. Find more tips here.
  • Rethink your social media usage. It’s okay to unfollow those who you don’t want to follow. Your account should be a safe and happy space for you to post and see whatever you want.
