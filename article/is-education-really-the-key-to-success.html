Is Education Really the Key to Success?

Society

09/13/20

According to dictionary.com, education is the process of imparting or acquiring general knowledge. Developing the power of reasoning and judgement. Preparing oneself intellectually for mature life. According to the second definition that pops up on Google, it’s an enlightening experience. (If these definitions don’t scream “liberation,” I don’t know what does.) We’ve all heard or seen the statistics on higher education: college graduates with a bachelor’s degree typically earn 66% more and are far less likely to face unemployment than those with just a high school diploma.[1] And if you’re reading this, you’re most likely in school, either applying for college or currently attending one, so some part of you probably believes that it’s worthwhile to get an education. And I’m just like you, someone who made my way through the K-12 system and is now taking on a four-year university, all in hopes of landing a stable and secure job that will let me live comfortably for the rest of my life. But through this lifelong, sometimes (edit: most of the time) stressful process, are we truly liberating ourselves and becoming enlightened, better people? Are we developing the skills to combat all of the structural -isms in the world, to think critically, and to equip ourselves to create a better tomorrow?

Brazilian philosopher Paulo Freire argues that we aren’t. Or at least, not in the way that we should be. In his 1968 book “Pedagogy of the Oppressed,” he discusses the “banking model of education,” in which the teacher is the epistemological source of authority and the students are passive, empty vessels, patiently waiting to store information passed down from their teachers. In this traditional Western model, Freire argues that students’ pre-existing knowledge is ignored and that their sole responsibility is to remember and recall the information provided to them. The world is “static and unchangeable, and students are simply supposed to fit into it as it is”.[2] If any of this sounds familiar to you, which it definitely does to me (see: AP Human Geography, or honestly any AP class in general), then you probably know how monotonous it can feel. Coupled with a culture of toxic competition that results in students dropping fine arts electives in favor of taking APs for GPA boosts, cheating, making spreadsheets to track class rank, and more, it’s not hard to believe that the true essence of education has gotten lost in the mix. But I’ll save that stuff for another blog post. The point is, education should free your mind, not numb it. The problem is that plenty of us are feeling numb and have been for a while.

So how do we wake ourselves up? According to the Norwegian Students’ and Academics’ International Assistance Fund (SAIH), education and liberation is not something you are given, but something you achieve through cognition and action. Freire proposes the problem-posing method of education as an alternative, which presents reality as it truly is, prioritizes dialogue between the student and his/her teacher, and emphasizes critical thinking as the ultimate pathway to liberation. In this model, the student’s prior knowledge can be utilized instead of ignored to reach even greater results. A similar pedagogy is problem-based learning, in which students learn through solving open-ended problems without defined solutions, allowing them to develop other skills in the process. In both cases, the student is put at the center of the process, leaving them better equipped to break free from the oppressive nature of the traditional education dynamic.

Ironically, I learned all of this from a course I’m currently taking in college. But I guess that really does show that education can be liberating, as long as we implement it in a way that promotes free exchange of thought. Only then can we truly begin the path to liberation.

Note: this article does not address structural barriers to education, such as poverty, race, and gender, nor does it address the idea that the education system is designed to divide the rich and the poor. That conversation itself deserves a separate article entirely.
