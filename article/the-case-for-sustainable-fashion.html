The Case for Sustainable Fashion

Society

08/02/20

As the clock strikes midnight on November 29, I rush to H&M’s website in hopes of taking advantage of the store’s Black Friday sales. While scouring the site for trendy clothes, I remained unaware of the fact that my business with H&M was contributing to their gross mistreatment of their garment workers. The $10 sweater I purchased was a direct product of the company’s exploitation and abuse of female labor [1]. Those $8 pants I bought helped promote a culture of poverty among workers. In fact, in a research study conducted by the Clean Clothes Campaign, H&M facilities pay less than 10% of the living wage to their Bulgarian workers [2].

Recently, the call to buy sustainable fashion has been amplified in light of the COVID-19 pandemic. Most notably, Kendall + Kylie (Kendall and Kylie Jenner’s fashion line) has been accused of severely underpaying their Bangladeshi factory workers[3]. Due to the unexpected situations surrounding COVID, Global Brands Group (GBG), a corporation that owns and collaborates with various well-known brands like TopShop & Revolve, cancelled all their Spring/Summer 2020 orders. GBG, which owns factories in Bangladesh, then used this as an excuse to withhold payment to their employees. The issue of underpaying workers is not limited to Kendall + Kylie, however. Other large fashion authorities like Zara use slave labor in their Argentina sweatshops[4]. Multi-billion dollar companies are paying their employees less than a dollar per hour. By exploiting marginalized groups abroad, these companies are committing modern-day colonialism. Not only do conglomerations exploit these workers financially, the factories they work in are dangerous. In 2013, this issue came to light when more than 1100 garment workers died in the collapse of Rana Plaza due to structural damage[5]. What happened to Rana Plaza shines light on the conditions of sweatshops around the world. Employees around the world are risking their lives for you and I to leisurely shop, and they are being financially robbed for it. We need to help them.

The problem with fast fashion lies not only on their labor practices but also their environmental impact. Companies often buy masses of cheap fabric to use and throw out, polluting the earth with waste. In 2016, textiles contributed to 1.2 billion tons of greenhouse gas emissions [6], and the fashion industry is responsible for around 10% of climate impact [7]. When factories discard tons of material at a time, they are severely contaminating the natural resources around them, impacting the living conditions of BIPOC who are more likely to live in such places. Again, this is another case of modern-day colonialism by the fashion industry!

Unfortunately, the transition from fast to slow fashion will (likewise) be slow. Our society is so deeply rooted in fast fashion that it will take years of public pressure for these practices to be reversed. Because most fast fashion brands are much more inexpensive than sustainable counterparts*, society has grown accustomed to sub-$10 clothing. Admittedly, when I first tried shopping sustainable and saw that a basic t-shirt was $30, I was shocked. Similar to me, many people often use price as an excuse to cop out of buying sustainably. The reality is that fast fashion has destroyed our perception of the true cost of clothing; we've grown familiar with the thin, low-quality feeling of fast fashion material in exchange for the cheaper price, and now, we must undo that.

Sustainability doesn’t have to be pricey. If you can afford designer brands, you have the financial resources to also support ethical fashion brands. While the shift in price mindset may be initially difficult, those extra dollars you are spending invest directly into a culture of fair wages, a cleaner earth, and long-term, higher-quality clothing. For those from lower socioeconomic backgrounds, the most popular sustainable alternative is thrifting/buying second-hand.** One thing to note though is that low-income communities are often excluded from the sustainable fashion movement. The suggestion above often comes from a place of class disconnect, and sustainable fashion brands should also work on making their products as affordable and accessible as possible (basic tees should not be $100). We won’t be able to truly reform fashion practices unless everyone participates, and a good starting point is buying sustainable basics.

Lastly, when shopping, we must also remember to promote intersectional sustainability. In response to environmental concerns mentioned earlier, fast fashion brands such as Zara and H&M have since released “limited-edition” or “exclusive” lines promoting eco-friendliness. However, the problem with this is that sustainability is not a trend. True sustainable fashion requires the revolution of the company’s entire philosophy. H&M Conscious line or Zara’s “Join Life” Campaign should not be chic alternatives but rather universal products. This ties into intersectionality/race because these large corporations are often led by non-marginalized people! BIPOC-owned businesses do not have nearly as much public presence as these companies do but have been practicing sustainability much longer. Thus, we should support minority-owned businesses while pressuring larger brands to reform. In the end, the goal is not to tear these companies down but rather rebuild them into something better.

When I was young, I never put too much thought into the “Made in Bangladesh” tag on my shirt. To me, that shirt was just another piece of clothing I wore for comfort. Yet, looking back now, a garment worker may have sacrificed their life for me to comfortably wear that shirt. We, as consumers, have the power to pay these sacrifices back, and it should be our duty to do so.

So what can YOU do?
1) Buy from ethical brands!
-To find ethical brands, visit Good on You, a site that provides detailed reviews to many big brands & suggests sustainable alternatives. Other resources include the Donegood Chrome Extension.
-Thrifting! See this post for a guide to ethical thrifting.
-Buy and sell second-hand clothes from Depop, Poshmark, Mercari, and Instagram closets
-Upcycle and donate clothes

2) Learn more about sustainable fashion:
-Follow @sustainablefashionforum, @cleanclothescampaign, @remakeourworld on Instagram
-Still unconvinced we need to improve textile labor conditions? Read about the 2013 Rana Plaza Collapse in Bangladesh.
-Another (more recent) article to read: Effects of COVID-19 on Garment Workers in Asia
-Watch True Cost documentary

3) Support garment workers’ unions & pressure brands to improve workers’ conditions!
-Sign Remake’s #PayUp Petition here
-Support the missions of Clean Clothes Campaign, Remake, Garment Workers Centre, & Asia Floor Wage Alliance

4) LAST BUT NOT LEAST, promoting sustainability does not only apply to the fashion industry! While this article focuses more on fashion, similar instances of marginalized communities being abused by the upper ruling class have been occurring in the food industry (Re: Driscolls’ underpaying farmworkers), e-commerce (Re: Amazon warehouse conditions), retail (Re: Walmart), and more. Before you purchase an item, try to conduct some research on the company and find (if applicable) better alternatives.

*Luxury brands such as Gucci/Versace, while expensive, are not excluded from “fast fashion.” Many high fashion brands continue to take advantage of garment workers in foreign countries. Rana Plaza Collapse is just one example of their involvement.

**I am fortunate enough to be born a POC with class privilege, so I am unable to speak on behalf of people from low-income backgrounds without fully understanding their struggles. However, I am learning more everyday and hope to share what I read with the world :)
