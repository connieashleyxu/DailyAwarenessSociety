5 Effects of the Capitol Hill Riot

Politics

01/10/21

January 6, 2021 started off as a normal day for many. Jon Ossoff and Reverend Raphael Warnock were just declared winners of the Georgia Senate runoffs, allowing Democrats to take control of the Senate, House, and White House. Kim Kardashian and Kanye West just announced their plans to divorce, and Congress started certifying President-Elect Biden’s victory. Yet, that afternoon, ardent Trump supporters raided the Capitol Building in order to overturn the results of the 2020 presidential election, transpiring hours of lockdown, chaos, and nationwide shock. Four days later, the events of that afternoon are still quite surreal to me, in particular how the current Trump administration and Capitol Police were so complacent to it. Let’s break down the aftermath of Tuesday afternoon.

  1. An early ending to Trump’s Presidency?
    As of today, 2 of Trump’s Cabinet members (Betsy Devos and Elaine Chao) and other White House aides like Mick Mulvaney have resigned from office. Twitter, Facebook, and other social media platforms have also indefinitely suspended Trump’s accounts. However, the left has been pushing for big tech to take more action on Trump’s social media behaviors since the start of his term. This delayed move may just be big tech trying to get on Democrats’ good side after the party seized control of the Senate. As the country transitions into a Democrat-majority House and Senate, Speaker of the House Nancy Pelosi has stated that the House is ready to impeach Trump if Vice President Pence does not invoke the 25th amendment, which would allow Pence to become President until Biden's inauguration. Senators and Representatives such as Ted Lieu, Cori Bush, AOC, and Bernie Sanders are also pushing to remove representatives who promoted election fraud.
  2. Conflicts within the GOP
    Following Tuesday’s riots, many Republican members of Congress who originally planned to dispute election results switched their stances on the topic (Ex: Kelly Loeffler). After members of Congress were barricaded in Capitol Hill for several hours, the certification process was even expedited, and notable members of the GOP such as Mitch McConnell and Mike Pence have recognized Biden’s victory for the first time since November. The narrative of election fraud spread by Trump could have potentially cost Republicans to lose their seats in Georgia during the runoff, instigating a divide between traditional Republicans and die-hard Trump supporters. Vice President Pence, for example, has started to reject pressure from Trump to continue spreading the narrative of election fraud, causing him to receive backlash from right-wing extremists.
  3. Rethinking the Social Dilemma (again)
    A few months ago, DAS published an article about the Netflix documentary The Social Dilemma here. Over the past couple months after watching the film, I’ve been noticing standoffs between Trump supporters and Democrats being recorded by both sides. Personally, my social feed has always leaned towards one side of the political spectrum, but I have wondered if the responses I see in the comment are similar on the other side. How out of touch am I from other political perspectives? Facebook and Twitter’s suspension of Trump’s accounts are only a start to moderating the fake news plaguing the Internet. While Trump and other Republican representatives and senators instigated the riot, far-right accounts on Parler or 4chan are much more harmful and grow the fire.
  4. Raising attention towards US imperialism worldwide
    While this was the first riot Americans have ever seen at the Capitol since the War of 1812, riots like these happen internationally, and they’re backed by our own country. History textbooks often glorify coups, which include overthrowing Nazi Germany and imperial Japan. However, much of the US’s attempts to change non-democratic regimes are not for human rights but rather to promote business interests or tap into natural resources, and both political parties in the US have supported coups worldwide. Currently, the US is heavily involved in Latin America, from Venezuela to Bolivia. In 2019, the US backed a 21-day political coup in Bolivia, leading to 33 deaths and 315 injuries, in order to take over the socialistic Bolivian government. We only saw a dumbed-down version of what this country enables in other places.

  5. Recognizing how prevalent white supremacy is
    In the past, I’ve always thought of members of groups like the Proud Boys and KKK to be white supremacists. Yet, Trump’s presidency has enabled a widespread complacency to white supremacy. Trump refused to condemn white supremacist groups at the presidential debate, instead telling them the Proud Boys to 'stand back and stand by.' After the riot, he still refused to call the National Guard and told rioters that they were valued and loved. The fact that there were still millions of people who voted for him is an indication that people condone his actions. The problem is not just limited to the President though. November’s election brought QAnon believers and neo-nazis to the House of Representatives. Videos showing some Capitol Police officers literally letting the gates open to rioters and posing selfies with them while government officials like Ted Cruz and Josh Hawley still promote the election fraud myth shows how some Americans find these behaviors acceptable. The fact that a country that prides itself on the stories of immigrants allows white supremacist behavior to hold government power is not okay.
