Is the American Dream Just a Facade?

Society, Equality

11/29/20

[1]

Coined in 1931 by historian James Truslow Adams in his book “Epic of America,” the American dream is an idea that the majority of Americans still believe in. But, according to a poll done by Gallup, the amount of Americans who believe the American dream is dwindling, and for good reason too.[2]

Let’s analyze the definition of “the American dream” as provided by the Merriam-Webster Dictionary. The first word that stands out to me is the word “anyone.” Immediately, a couple of questions come to mind:

1) How feasible is it for anyone to be living the American dream?
2) At this time, is the American dream just a facade?

1) How feasible is it for anyone to be living the American dream?

In the U.S., only 3% of the population has a net worth of $1 million.[3] This statistic begs the question, why is that percentage not greater if anyone can achieve the American dream and gain wealth by just working hard?

It’s no secret that privilege and luck plays a huge part in achieving monetary success. Americans are born into different playing fields in their race to reach the American dream. Any type of discrimination can influence the probability an individual has of becoming a millionaire.

The following quiz was built off of Bloomberg’s article that determines the odds someone may have based on their race, education, and age.[4] The article and quiz only provide a probability for a select amount of races (White, Asian, Black, and Hispanic) due to the limited information received from research results.

After completing the quiz, try to remember your results.



It’s no secret that becoming a millionaire is difficult, so most percentages are rather low. Did you think the percentages you received were “fair”? Let’s take compare the percentages with one another graphically. The following graphs are from Bloomberg’s article.

The only factor in the quiz that could be changed seemingly* by choice of attending higher-level education marginally change prospects of becoming a millionaire for Blacks and Hispanics; Blacks and Hispanics have disproportionately lower chances of becoming millionaires regardless of their education.

In the quiz, there were a total of 60 results that one may receive with all the configurations of the answer choices. However, in all 60 possibilities, Blacks and Hispanics were always below the mean percentage of their prospects to become a millionaire.

In essence, it is not equally feasible for everyone to be living the American dream.

So, this begs the question: at this time, is the American dream just a facade?

The answer to this question is still unclear and up to your interpretation.

But, it is clear systemic racism still plagues the US. While we are hearing more voices in the media about these deep-rooted issues, we aren’t seeing enough changes to make a lasting impact.

The American dream doesn’t have to be a facade if we take reformative actions to put an end to racism in our country.
