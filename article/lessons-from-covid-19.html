19 Lessons From COVID-19

Society

08/30/20

Today marks nearly 6 months since the start of quarantine. I still remember that last day before spring break when things were “normal.” That morning, you could hear my frustrations from my dorm room. I was struggling to pack my belongings. Somehow, I had caught a cold the night before. I quickly took some Tylenol, and before I could say one last goodbye to my friends, I was gone, hoping to go home quickly to rest. Clueless, I had no idea that that would be the start to half a year in isolation.

I still remember when COVID-19 just appeared on headlines. My friends and I often discussed it. We became slightly wary of our families’ Chinese grocery store purchases, but we denied that it would ever grow to the scale it is now. We initially thought that the virus would stay in China, but then it spread to Italy and South Korea. Suddenly, a few cases popped up in Seattle, but that’s ok -- it won’t ever come to where we are right? Oh, were we wrong.

In February, I recall thinking my mom was unreasonable for buying a mask. “Just in case,” she said. Only a few weeks later did I get an email announcing that school would be online for the rest of the semester. A few months later, history repeated itself yet again when I received an email declaring my first year of college would be held in a hybrid, mostly online, setting. Mom knows best, I guess.

While this pandemic has been truly unprecedented, it has taught us several lessons about our world, ourselves, and our science. Rather than regretting our past, we can only reflect and grow. Here are 19 things I learned from COVID-19:

1) The definition of privilege. These past few months, I have been lucky enough to have a safe shelter and a loving family. I am also able to afford the extra costs of food and utility that come with staying at home. Yet, I can only imagine what those less fortunate than I am are going through, especially for those who lost their jobs, had their salaries paused, or live in a place where their mental and/or physical health is compromised. These aspects of life have never meant much to me, but now I recognize they are tokens of my privilege.

2) The earlier, the better. Over these six months, the thought of “I wish this pandemic was over” has crossed almost everyone’s minds. With the fatigue and loneliness that comes with staying at home, people are then less inclined to social distance, making it more difficult to contain the disease. However, if we all stayed home for a couple of weeks at the beginning of the pandemic, then I believe that everyone would be able to go out more safely now, but no use in regretting! Just a lesson for the future :)

3) The smallest things mean so much. I looked through my camera roll at least 30 times over quarantine. Once, I got all teary-eyed looking at a picture of a sunset from last summer. Just by looking at it, I was transported back to that day and could feel the warm air and breeze in my face. I never knew landscape could make one so emotional.

4) Time taken to reflect upon yourself is invaluable. This quarantine may be the first time you had such a long period to yourself. Pre-COVID, I was constantly surrounded by others that I occasionally forgot about who I am. Journal! Lay in bed while staring into the ceiling! Vibe to music! Thinking about who you are and what you want to be regardless of external factors. This is your life, not anyone else’s.

5) Self-care boosts your confidence and serotonin levels by so much! Whenever I took a shower, did skincare, cleaned my room, or made a snack, I felt more productive and happier with myself. I always thought these tasks were “small,” but don’t undermine their power!

6) Take care of the earth. A positive result of this pandemic has been the cleaner air due to less traffic. I’ve also had the opportunity to take a lot more walks outside in quarantine, and it’s extremely relaxing to appreciate nature, strengthening my connection with it. However, as lockdowns ease, I’m afraid that will result in even more traffic and pollution, reversing the positive effects.

7) The pandemic created the perfect environment for us to learn. One of the most notable features of 2020 was how everything negative seemed to conglomerate in one year. Police brutality came into light, shining on systemic racism in nearly every part of our lifestyle. With that, everyone (stuck at home) became more inclined to learn. While there were a lot of terrible things that happened in 2020, one positive thing was the willingness many took to be better.

8) Hobbies should be something fun, even without friends. Pre-pandemic, every time I was bored, I would go hang out with my friends, but now, I can’t call my friends or be on my device 24/7. Hobbies are what I genuinely enjoy doing on my own. No external influences involved.

9) Our world is interconnected. Just think about it! The virus spread halfway across the world to essentially everywhere in a matter of months. All it took were a few plane rides and cargo shipments. If that doesn’t perfectly show what globalization is, I don’t know what will.

10) Because we are so interconnected, that means we are all one big community! The virus doesn’t care if you’re the President or a common citizen. We have to remember to take care of everyone. We won’t get through this without anyone being left behind.

11) America is so deeply rooted in capitalism. Back in March, shopping for PPE with limited supplies and increasing prices was a perfect example of how the free-market is harmful to society. Not everyone could afford to stay safe in the time we all needed to be.

12) Time zones are overrated. Sometimes, I have to jump on a call in another time zone, and I can’t count how many times I forget to convert it to my time. I just don’t see the point that extra hassle.

13) The true meaning of a family. It’s ok to be annoyed with your family. In fact, I think it’s perfectly normal. Admittedly, my family often barges in during my Zoom calls or screams my name when I am in a call, and I can’t quantify or put into words how irritated I get. Yet, in the end, those little quirks are what makes us family, and whenever I leave for college, it’s these things I look back on and smile. That’s family for you.

14) Meeting new people was hard already. Now it’s even worse over Zoom.

15) Everything changed so quickly. The world adapted to wearing masks in a few months. I can’t even remember what it’s like being in a crowded area anymore. Seeing a picture of one sends me chills.

16) Can life truly ever return to “normal"? I think that some methodologies we developed due to COVID-19 should carry on to our life even when the virus (hopefully) is contained, such as wearing a mask when you’re sick or public places cleaning routinely after people come. Hygiene is never a bad thing, and this pandemic just made us more aware of it.

17) Science and humanities are intertwined, not separate. Up until probably my senior year of high school, I always thought I had to “choose” a field. STEM or humanities. Business or technology. However, recently, I’ve recognized that you never really have to choose because each field adopts from other ones. For example, this pandemic, on eye level, is just epidemiology and finding effective treatments, but there’s more to it than just science. Studying the social sciences allows us to find how is the treatment going to be equally distributed, how can we take care of marginalized communities, and more.

18) Social media can be a toxic place. Whenever I see a post/story of others outside, I am more inclined to stop caring about the virus (which, obviously, is a poor decision). Seeing others share problematic opinions or take part in performative activism has also been harmful as my feelings get riled up. It’s okay to take a step back.

19) Last but not least, we really need to vote Trump out. In case Trump’s misogyny, racism, and transphobia were not enough of a reason to go out there and vote in November, our “President” has handled the pandemic in the worst ways possible. It was first promoting the consumption of bleach/cleaning products for me. Then it was the boasting that we can detain the virus by Easter but refusing to wear a mask for me. Then it was rallying his supporters to believe COVID-19 is a hoax for me. I could go on and on, but you get it. This pandemic has shown us that our “President” can’t even protect our lives.

As colleges reopen across the country, we can only hope that the virus will not breed on campuses. This pandemic is not a partisan issue. No one wants to get sick, but SARS-COV-2 does not pick and choose who to infect based on race, ethnicity, gender, etc. However, by wearing a mask and following social distancing guidelines for just a few weeks, soon enough this article will be a piece of the past.

So what can you do?

- WEAR A MASK. PLEASE. According to a paper published in The Lancet, any type of face-covering decreased the rate of COVID-19 transmission by nearly 5 times.[1] Pairing a facial covering with social distancing makes your risk of catching the virus virtually zero. Recently, a Duke University study found that neck gaiters may be more harmful than wearing no mask at all.[2] However, it’s important to note that this study was flawed.[3] They only tested ONE neck gaiter, which is not enough to discount all of them. If this is the only type of face-covering you have, please still wear it outside. Read here on types of effective face coverings.

- Social distance (6 feet apart) when possible! If you are ever bored at home, reference our previous blog post about things to do in quarantine (self-care edition) plus our Instagram thread on easy habits to pick up in quarantine.

- Read about the racial and income equality of the virus. Black and Latinx people are 3 times more likely to catch COVID-19.[4] Navajo Nation, the White Mountain Apache Tribe, and other Native American tribes have the highest infection rates in the country, passing that of New York state.[5] Socioeconomic status was also found to be a coronavirus risk factor.[6] In this graphic, you can see how low-income communities in New York City have been impacted the most by the pandemic.

- Donate masks/PPE to incarcerated populations and ICE detention centers. Incarcerated populations are more than 5 times more likely to die from the virus.[7] In an article published in early August, COVID-19 conditions in ICE detention centers were extremely unsanitary as ICE officials do not wear masks or respect quarantine. In fact, 20 percent of detainees have tested positive, and ICE has underreported cases.[8] Unfortunately, information regarding the conditions in both these places have been murky.

- Vote. Vote for adequate healthcare for all. Vote for people who trust our nation’s scientists. Our choices now directly impact our futures. Visit vote.org to request an absentee ballot; it literally takes less than two minutes, and you can vote safely from home.

- Tip the waiters a little bit more when you go out and eat. Essential workers are sacrificing their lives every time they go to work so that they can afford to stay safe and healthy during this time. We need to do more for them.
