Sellout Culture

Society

11/15/20

Roughly half of Harvard’s Class of 2017 who entered the workforce after graduation worked in the consulting, finance, and technology industries. Yet, when the same class was surveyed as incoming freshmen, only a fifth said they planned on working in those industries after graduation. [1]

What happened in those four years?

"Selling out" is a common expression that describes the compromising of a person's integrity, morality, authenticity, or principles in exchange for personal gain, such as money. [2] Although it can be applied in the context of politics, music, art, and entertainment, this week’s article focuses more specifically on how it operates on college campuses.

Most of us have probably (or are currently) grappling with this dichotomy as we enter the world of higher education, in which we must decide between various degree pathways, navigate taxing internship and recruitment seasons, and balance passion vs profit and impact vs prestige. And to make matters worse, the environment in which all of this occurs — the college campuses themselves — is evolving due to the overpowering influence of top consulting and finance firms (think Goldman Sachs, McKinsey, Morgan Stanley, etc). Indra Sofian from Medium writes that “[o]ver decades, these firms have thoroughly and meticulously infiltrated and transformed the culture of these universities, resulting in a staggeringly high conversion rate of graduates into their ranks. As a result, fewer candidates are left for other fields that need them, such as healthcare, education, energy, and environmental science. It’s a very real — and very damaging — brain drain.” [3] Lured by promises of prestige, success, and safety, we gravitate towards careers that guarantee stability without considering the ethical implications and personal sacrifices that they demand. Sofian also writes: “Entire student organizations — like the undergraduate consulting club or a student investment committee — are created to shuttle high-performers into these companies. Concentrations and majors like economics are major pipelines into consulting and finance, which results in disproportionate numbers of students pursuing these fields of study at top universities.” [3] A 2017 article from the Harvard Crimson writes that “for seniors being coaxed by a silver platter of options from proactive private sector firms, the anxiety to get a job as quickly and painlessly as possible often leads to corporate capitulation. And combined with the reassuring structure of recruitment, the job security represented by a Bain office in a Manhattan skyscraper usually trumps the unsexy idealism of an unkempt nonprofit office in Lansing.” [4]

Of course, it’s important to note that sellout culture cannot be discussed without considering the structural context in which it occurs: a combination of a capitalistic and profit-driven society that values certain types of bodies, identities, and experiences over others. The two go hand-in-hand, and achieving such a culture change — one that permeates the campus of every high-ranking university — will require the efforts of not only the students and administration directly involved, but also the dismantling of the institutions that uphold it in the first place.
