Highlights From 2020 Election Week

Politics

11/08/20

Election week has been long and nerve-wracking for many Americans including myself. It was filled with constant browser refreshing, stress, and surprises. Here are some notable events from this past week, aside from the Biden-Harris victory!

  • Cori Bush became the very first Black woman elected to Congress in Missouri. She is a progressive Democrat, nurse, activist, and single mother. On Election Night, Bush stated “As the first Black woman and also the first nurse and single mother to have the honor to represent Missouri in the United States Congress, let me say this: To the Black women, the Black girls, the nurses, the essential workers, the single mothers, this is our moment.” In 2014 after the shooting of unarmed Black teenager Michael Brown, she led protests in Ferguson.[1][2]
  • Ritchie Torres and Mondaire Jones will be the first openly gay Black members of Congress. Professor Reynolds, a professor at Princeton University’s School of Public and International Affairs, stated that Torres and Jones’ victories represent “a significant moment of psychological change in who we feel is electable as a politician.” Congress now has 9 LGBTQ members.[14]
  • Sarah McBride became the first openly trans person elected to the state Senate, making her the United States’ highest-ranking openly trans official. She has focused on LGBTQ equality and has worked at the Human Rights Campaign and Equality Delaware. In 2013, McBride pushed for the Gender Identity Nondiscrimination Act which ensured equal legal protections to trans people in Delaware.[3][4][7]
  • Every member of “the Squad” was reelected. The members are Ayanna Pressley representing Massachusetts District 7, Alexandria Ocasio-Cortez representing New York District 14, Rashida Tlaib representing Michigan District 13, and Ilhan Omar representing Minnesota District 5. These women of color are known for their work in progressive issues such as the Green New Deal which addressed climate change and economic inequality.[6]
  • Michele Rayner-Goolsby became the first openly Black LGBTQ woman elected to the Florida House.

  • Taylor Small became Vermont’s first openly trans legislator. She is the director of the Vermont Pride Center’s Health and Wellness program. Small is passionate about supporting the LGBTQ community of Vermont and increasing queer and trans visibility. She’s also participated in LGBTQ events such as Queen City Drag Cabaret and her drag persona name is Nikki Champagne.[7]
  • Stephanie Byers became Kansas’s first openly trans state lawmaker. In 2018, GLSEN (American education organization dedicated to ending discrimination against LGBTQ students) named Byers National Educator of the Year.[7][8]
  • Mauree Turner became the first Muslim person elected to Oklahoma state legislature as well as the first non-binary state lawmaker in the country. The president and CEO for LGBTQ Victory Fund, Annise Parker, stated “Mauree is now a trailblazer for non-binary people and their courage to run openly will inspire more non-binary people to pursue careers in elected office.” Turner has spent her life fighting for “immigration rights, racial justice, and criminal justice.”[9]
  • Many Americans were worried about 300,000 missing mail-in ballots, however an article from Vox explains how this was not the case. The President of the American Postal Workers Union, Mark Dimondstein, stated that “Just because something didn’t have [a delivery scan] does not translate at all into ‘it was not delivered.” He explained that the ballots were treated with even higher priority than first-class mail and were given expedited delivery with the goal of getting the ballots mailed in a “quick and efficient” manner.[5]
  • Nevada became the 1st state in the US to protect gay marriage in its constitution. The removal of Nevada’s old provision stating that marriage would only be recognized between a man and woman means that even if gay marriage rights were to change at a federal level, same-sex marriage rights would remain protected in Nevada.[10]
  • There was an unprecedented number of mail-in ballots this year, leading to delays in election results. Nevada became a meme for its pace of vote-counting. Government officials in Nevada explained that this year they are focusing on accuracy over speed.   • Republican women have defied expectations by gaining seats in the House. The Washington Post reports that “of the 8 House seats that Republicans flipped so far [as of Nov. 7], six were won by female candidates.” Representative-elect Nancy Mace of South Carolina said, “It’s not just Democrat women breaking glass ceilings; we broke a huge barrier Tuesday night, Wednesday morning, when the result was called in South Carolina.”[13]
  • Voter turnout reached record highs this election year. On November 4th, Bloomberg stated that 43 states displayed higher turnout levels than that of the 2016 election. This year, Minnesota grew from 2016’s 73% turnout among those who can vote to 81%. States including Florida, North Carolina, Vermont, and Wisconsin have also displayed over 70% turnout.[15]
  • A prominent Republican election lawyer, Benjamin L. Ginsberg, called out President Trump’s attempt to to prevent voters from being able to vote in the United States. Ginsberg explains that the Trump campaign did not try to “make it easier for citizens to vote”, but instead “[sought] to erect barriers.” These barriers Ginsberg discusses include “seeking to block state laws to ease voting during the pandemic” and “challenging the ballots of individual voters unlikely to support him.[17]
  • Election night was followed by protesters “demanding a tally of all votes in the US election”, insisting that the counting of votes stop. These protests arose with claims that there were issues with the counting of votes. Police arrested over 600 protesters in Minneapolis on Wednesday night for “protesting Trump’s threats to challenge the election results.” On Thursday morning, Trump supporters gathered outside the Phoenix city hall chanting “Protect Our Vote.”[16]
