Biden's Cabinet Picks Need More Diversity

Politics

12/06/2020

Over the past few weeks, President-Elect Biden has announced several of his Cabinet picks, some of which will be historical nominations. Janet Yellen, former Chair of the Federal Reserve, has been nominated as the next Secretary of Treasury and will be the first woman in that position. While we should celebrate such achievements, there’s still much work to be done. Biden’s promise he made to “select a Cabinet that represents America” has not held true. Much of Biden’s nominees for some of this country’s most powerful positions are white and upper-class, a stark contrast to the true demographics of the US.


Source: CNN

Currently, Biden's cabinet consists of UN Ambassador nominee Linda Thomas-Greenfield, a Black woman, Secretary of Homeland Security nominee Alejandro Mayorkas, the first Latino to serve in the position, and Neera Tanden, the first woman of color and South Asian person Office of Management and Budget director. Cecilia Rouse is also the first woman of color to serve as the chair of the Council of Economic Advisers.[1]

Minority voters were the voices that lifted Biden to victory. According to exit polls, Asian, Black, Latino, and other ethnic minorities all favored Biden while a majority of White voters favored Trump.[2] Black grassroots organizers such as Stacey Abrams, Helen Butler, Tamieka Atkins, and more deserve credit for registering 800,000+ Georgia voters, pushing Georgia blue. For these groups, a vote for Biden meant a step towards equality and acceptance. It meant having our voices finally heard. Representation for these groups at the highest levels of government are crucial. We need the Biden-Harris administration to nominate a diverse cabinet so that the voices of all of America heard. Yet, the current picks to this point have been so disappointing that Black, Latino, and AAPI groups such as the NAACP, Congressional Hispanic Caucus, and Congressional Asian Pacific American Caucus have been forced to write to President-Elect Biden to encourage him to fill more high-profile positions with minorities. As a response, Biden has said, "I'm not going to tell you now exactly what I'm doing in any department, but I promise you, it'll be the single most diverse cabinet, based on race, color, based on gender, that's ever existed in the United States of America.”

There are still several key cabinet positions remaining, including Secretary of Education, Secretary of Health and Human Services, Attorney General, and Secretary of Justice. For many, voting for Biden meant voting for a President and administration that would genuinely speak for them both economically and socially. Having a Cabinet member that represents various demographics means picking someone who knows the problems these groups of people face and has the power to fix them. A Hispanic nominee for HHS Secretary, for example, is important as COVID-19 has disproportionately rampaged in Black and Latinx communities. New Mexico Governor Michelle Lujan Grisham has been a favorite among Hispanic and Latino lawmakers for this spot as she was one of the first leaders to enact a stay-at-home order, close schools, and pass a stimulus bill in her state when the disease spread in March.[3]

It’s important to note that diversity is not only limited to ethnicity and race, but also gender, economic class, sexual orientation, and political view. Progressives such as AOC and Ilhan Omar notably held Among Us Twitch streams to push young voters to get out and vote. All of these groups mobilized millions of voters in order to put Biden in the White House. As January and Biden’s inauguration date nears, I hope to see a true America reflected in Biden’s top Cabinet picks, signaling that he values the people who helped him throughout his campaign.
