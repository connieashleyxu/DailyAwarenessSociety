Three Filmmakers You Should Know

Arts & Entertainment

10/11/20

AVA DUVERNAY

If you’ve been on Instagram at all in the past few months amidst an outpouring of posts about social change, you’ve probably heard of the Netflix documentary 13th (2016), a groundbreaking film that explores the issue of systemic racism in America, the era of mass incarceration, the prison-industrial complex, and the evolution of slavery. You’ve probably heard of When They See Us (2019), another Netflix docuseries that follows the lives of the Central Park Five, a group of teens of color who were wrongfully convicted of rape and assault. But what you might not know is that these two films were the brainchildren of the same filmmaker: Ava DuVernay.

A writer, producer, director, and distributor of independent film, DuVernay is critically acclaimed and historically relevant. She was the first black woman to be nominated for a Golden Globe for Best Director for Selma (2014), and she is the highest grossing black woman director in American box office history, made possible by her work on Disney’s A Wrinkle in Time (2018). Her films are gripping, empowering, revolutionary, and beautifully made; they tackle issues of race, class, and power and have transformed the conversations I’ve been having recently about the current state of America. DuVernay’s work resonates strongly with me as someone avidly curious about politics and social change, but also as an appreciator of cinematography, aesthetics, and production. I highly recommend looking into her work, and I’m currently making my way through her filmography!

WES ANDERSON

Very few filmmakers have mastered the ability to create their own distinct style — one that is instantly recognizable from just a singular shot — like Wes Anderson; in fact, his unique approach has even been deemed the "Wes Anderson style" of movie-making, intensely studied by film students and appreciators alike. Of his filmography, Moonrise Kingdom (2012) and The Grand Budapest Hotel (2014) are personal favorites (although I still haven’t seen all of them), not only for their aesthetics but also for their writing. Wes Anderson's movies are visually, aesthetically, and cinematically pleasing. Some of his easily recognizable trademarks include the use of symmetry, pastel colors, and profile shots of his actors and actresses. SC Lannom writes in StudioBinder's "The Wes Anderson Style Explained" that his "aesthetic is much more deliberate [than what initially meets the eye]. It seems simple, but it's complex. The viewer gets to enjoy a sophisticated film that spoon-feeds information. His stories are undeniably fun, and he is one of the only working directors who can pull off massive tonal shifts." [1] He is regarded by some as the modern-day version of the auteur, a filmmaker or director with a recognizable style or thematic preoccupation.

It's also important to note that Wes Anderson isn't a cinematographer but a director; the credit for all of his iconic shots should be given to Robert Yeoman, the sole cinematographer Anderson has worked with for all of his live-action films. As Lannom describes in the article, "Anderson and Yeoman often present images in symmetrical, flat compositions. This furthers the 'storybook motif' that Anderson aims to create with his visuals. Viewers feel like they are moving through some sort of doll house world. The imagery in Wes Anderson films can be light and fluffy, but it's presented in a direct, succinct, and precise manner. This gives it style and weight." Wes Anderson is truly a movie-making genius, and I highly recommend following his work. (He's also a UT alum!)

JORDAN PEELE

Many knew him from his sketch comedy series Key and Peele, but most know him now from his critically acclaimed filmography, which includes Get Out (2017), BlacKkKlansman (2018), and Us (2019). Jordan Peele, the mastermind of horror and comedy, must also be included in this week’s spotlight.

Peele has only been in the world of directing and producing for a few years (although he acted and starred in several TV series years earlier), making his success and talent as a relatively new filmmaker all the more impressive. Get Out, his directorial debut, received immense praise, nominations, and awards (as it deserved); it scored a 98% on Rotten Tomatoes and was nominated for Best Picture, Best Director, Best Original Screenplay, and Best Actor at the Oscars. Peele has a unique ability to seamlessly tie in themes of race, class, culture, and comedy into his horror films, leaving his audience Googling the plot after the movie has ended in wonder and shock. The Independent writes that "[m]ixing horror and comedy is hardly a new idea, but there are few directors who can so thoroughly entertain an audience while also delivering layered, nuanced social commentary – the kind that keeps people talking long after the screams subside." These nuances — which primarily comment on the state of America and the role of race, whiteness, and blackness — are laced throughout his films; an example is The Sunken Place in Get Out, whose "metaphorical meaning [is] commonly used now to describe black individuals who appear to be willingly upholding and promoting racist constructs and ideologies." [2] I love dissecting, analyzing, and reading about these concepts afterwards, because they're incredibly complex, more than they initially seem. In an interview about Us (2019), Peele said, "We are our own worst enemy. Not just as individuals but more importantly as a group, as a family, as a society, as a country, as a world. We are afraid of the shadowy, mysterious ‘other’ that’s gonna come and kill us and take our jobs... no one’s taking responsibility for where we’re at." As a director and storyteller, Peele has mastered the art of manipulating his audiences as each element of the plot unfolds, while simultaneously challenging their preconceived notions of the world.
